Animal Husbandry – Class 9 Biology

Learn how scientific care of animals increases milk, eggs, meat, fish and honey production, while keeping animals healthy and safe.

What is Animal Husbandry?

Meaning · Importance · Scope

Animal husbandry is the scientific management of farm animals for food and other products through proper feeding, housing, breeding and disease control.

It includes cattle, buffalo, sheep, goat, poultry and fish farming, which together supply milk, meat, eggs and other animal products to a growing population.

With rising population and better living standards, the demand for animal products is increasing, so livestock production must be planned scientifically and efficiently.

There is also increasing awareness that animals should be treated humanely, so modern farms focus on comfort, cleanliness and low‑stress handling of animals.

Feeding
Balanced rations and feed additives improve growth, reproduction and product quality.
Breeding
Controlled and cross‑breeding help develop high‑yield, disease‑resistant varieties.
Health Care
Vaccination, deworming and hygiene protect animals from major diseases and parasites.
Did you know? Animal husbandry provides regular income, employment and organic manure, and supports integrated farming with crops.
In a nutshell: Animal husbandry makes livestock farming scientific, productive and humane by combining good housing, feeding, breeding and health‑care practices.

Cattle Farming

Milk · Draught Power · Breeds

Purpose: Cattle are reared for milk (milch animals) and for farm work like ploughing, irrigation and carting (draught animals).

Breeds and Cross‑breeding

Indian cattle belong to two species: Bos indicus (cows) and Bos bubalis (buffaloes). Females that produce milk are called milch animals, while strong males and some females used for work are called draught animals.

Milk yield depends partly on the lactation period, the period after calving during which milk is produced. Longer lactation means more total milk.

CategoryIndigenous BreedsExotic Breeds
MilchRed Sindhi, Sahiwal, GirJersey, Holstein Friesian, Brown Swiss
DraughtAmrit Mahal, Kankrej, Hallikar— (mainly Indian used)

Exotic breeds generally have higher milk yield and longer lactation, while Indian breeds show better disease resistance and adaptability to local climate.

Cross‑breeding combines these advantages, producing animals that are disease‑resistant, heat‑tolerant and high milk producers.

Housing and Hygiene

Cattle should be housed in well‑ventilated, spacious sheds that protect them from rain, heat and cold. A gentle slope in the floor allows urine and wash water to drain away.

Regular brushing removes dirt and loose hair, improving blood circulation and keeping the skin healthy. Clean bedding and separate areas for feeding and dung help maintain hygiene.

Feeding and Rations

Maintenance ration
Food needed for basic life processes—breathing, movement and normal activity.
Milk‑producing ration
Extra high‑energy and high‑protein feed given during lactation to support milk yield.
Types of feed
Roughage (green and dry fodder) plus concentrates (oil cakes, grains, bran, mineral mix).

Balanced rations supply carbohydrates, proteins, fats, minerals, vitamins and enough clean water. Mineral mixture and vitamin supplements act as feed additives to enhance health and production.

Diseases and Control

Cattle can suffer from external parasites on the skin and internal parasites like worms and flukes in the stomach or liver, which reduce growth and milk production.

They are also attacked by infectious diseases caused by bacteria and viruses. Regular deworming, timely vaccination and prompt treatment of sick animals are essential.

Key idea: High milk production comes from good breeds, long lactation, balanced feeding, clean housing and strict disease control.

Poultry Farming

Layers · Broilers · Management

Poultry farming raises domestic fowl for egg production (layers) and chicken meat (broilers).

Breeds and Traits

TypeIndigenous ExampleExotic Example
LayerAseelWhite Leghorn
BroilerKadaknathRhode Island Red

Cross‑breeding between indigenous and exotic birds improves traits such as number and quality of chicks, early maturity, fast growth and better survival.

Improved birds are selected for tolerance to high summer temperatures, low maintenance requirements and ability to use cheaper, fibrous feed prepared from agricultural by‑products.

Egg vs Broiler Production

AspectLayers (Eggs)Broilers (Meat)
Main objectiveMaximum number of good‑quality eggsRapid body weight gain and soft meat
RationBalanced feed with emphasis on minerals and vitamins for egg shell and yolkProtein‑rich feed with extra fat, high vitamins A and K
Rearing periodStart laying around 20 weeks; productive up to ~72 weeksMarketed at about 6–8 weeks of age

Housing and Health

Poultry houses should be dry, well‑ventilated and protected from extreme heat, cold and rain. Proper stocking density prevents overcrowding and helps birds grow evenly.

Clean water, fresh feed, regular cleaning of droppings, and spraying of disinfectants reduce disease risk. Litter should be kept dry to avoid fungal infections.

Poultry may suffer from diseases caused by viruses, bacteria, fungi, parasites and from nutritional deficiencies. Systematic vaccination and bio‑security measures protect the flock.

Did you know? Poultry farming gives quick returns because broilers reach market size in less than two months.
Remember: Improved breeds + balanced ration + hygienic housing + vaccination = profitable, low‑mortality poultry farm.

Fish Production

Capture vs Culture · Marine · Inland

Fish and shellfish are rich, cheap sources of animal protein. Fish production includes both capture fishing and culture fishery.

In capture fishing, fish are obtained directly from natural water bodies. In culture fishery, fish are reared in controlled water bodies like ponds, tanks and reservoirs.

Marine Fisheries

India has about 7500 km of coastline and vast deep‑sea areas. Common marine fish are pomfrets, mackerels, tunas, sardines and Bombay duck.

Fish are caught using different nets from mechanised boats. Modern devices such as echo‑sounders and satellites help locate large schools of fish in the sea.

High‑value marine organisms like mullets, bhetki, pearl spots, prawns, mussels and oysters are also farmed in seawater. Culture of such marine organisms is called mariculture. Oysters are additionally cultivated for pearls.

Inland Fisheries & Composite Fish Culture

Fresh‑water resources include ponds, rivers, canals and reservoirs, while brackish waters like estuaries and lagoons are also important for fish production.

Most inland yield comes from aquaculture. Sometimes fish culture is combined with paddy cultivation so that fish grow in the water of paddy fields.

In composite fish culture, several species are stocked together in one pond: Catla (surface feeder), Rohu (middle‑zone feeder), Mrigal and Common Carp (bottom feeders) and Grass Carp (weed feeder). This ensures full use of food available at all levels of the pond without competition.

A major problem is shortage of good‑quality fish seed, because many species breed naturally only during monsoon and wild seed may contain unwanted species.

This problem is solved by inducing fish to breed in ponds using hormonal stimulation, which provides pure seed of desired species in required quantity.

Exam line: “Composite fish culture and induced breeding increase fish yield by using all food zones and by ensuring continuous supply of pure fish seed.”

Bee‑Keeping (Apiculture)

Honey · Wax · Apiaries

Bee‑keeping is a low‑investment enterprise that provides farmers extra income from honey and beeswax while improving pollination of crops.

Bee Species

SpeciesCommon NameFeatures
Apis cerana indicaIndian beeCommonly kept in hives, moderate honey yield.
Apis dorsataRock beeVery high honey yield, wild, difficult to domesticate.
Apis floraeLittle beeSmall, low honey yield.
Apis melliferaItalian beeHigh honey yield, gentle, stays longer in hive, breeds well.

Apiaries and Pasturage

For commercial honey production, many hives are kept together in bee farms called apiaries, usually near orchards or fields rich in flowers.

The availability of flowering plants providing nectar and pollen is called pasturage. Both the quantity and type of flowers influence the yield and taste of honey.

Why Italian Bees?

High honey capacity
They collect and store more honey than local bees.
Mild nature
They sting less aggressively, so easier to handle.
Good hive behaviour
They stay longer in the same hive and breed rapidly.
Core idea: Selecting good bee species, placing apiaries in areas with rich pasturage and careful hive management result in large quantities of high‑quality honey and wax.

  • Poultry farming develops layers and broilers with traits like fast growth, early maturity and high egg or meat yield.

  • Fish production uses capture fishing and culture fishery in marine and inland waters.

  • Bee‑keeping uses Indian and Italian bees; honey quality depends on pasturage.
  • Animal husbandry increases food supply, farmer income and employment while promoting scientific and humane care of animals.
